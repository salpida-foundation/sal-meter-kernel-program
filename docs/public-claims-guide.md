## 1. Why This Page Exists

The current stage of the program is fragile.

That fragility is not only technical.

It is also linguistic.

A project like this can be damaged when people begin saying things such as:

- “the hard part is already solved”
- “the repository proves enough”
- “this already measures consciousness”
- “this is clinically meaningful now”
- “this is already a validated public platform”
- “this is basically equivalent to canonical CAIS / Sal-Meter”
- “this system is compliant because it resembles the architecture”

Those statements are not merely premature.

They distort the actual burden.

This page exists to keep public language aligned with the current stage:

- pre-opening
- kernel-first
- research-stage
- non-therapeutic
- boundary-conscious
- evidence-disciplined

---

## 2. The Core Rule

The core rule is simple:

**public language must never outrun the strongest thing the current evidence and authority layers can actually support.**

That means public claims must remain:

- stage-accurate
- bounded
- non-inflationary
- non-clinical unless canonically and evidentially justified
- non-compliance-implying unless canonically and evidentially justified
- careful about the difference between:
  - research-stage exploration
  - validated result
  - canonical definition
  - designation right
  - compliance status
  - public interpretation

A good public claim narrows misunderstanding.

A bad public claim widens the meaning of the project faster than the work can carry it.

---

## 3. The First Public-Language Principle

The first public-language principle is this:

**Do not speak as if the strongest future vision is already the current achieved state.**

This is the easiest mistake to make.

The architecture may be ambitious.
The long-term relevance may be large.
The civilizational implications may be serious.

That still does **not** authorize present-tense overclaiming.

The current public-language baseline should stay close to formulations such as:

- early-stage
- research-stage
- exploratory
- bounded
- non-therapeutic
- state-sensitive
- signal-interface
- protocol-and-validation focused
- pre-opening
- kernel-first

These phrases preserve reality.

That is their value.

## 4. What the Project May Be Publicly Called Right Now

At the current stage, public-facing language may appropriately describe the project as:

- a kernel-first research program
- a research-stage measurement architecture
- a bounded CAIS-aligned signal-interface effort
- a non-therapeutic state-sensitive signal program
- a pre-opening Sal-Meter / CAIS kernel program
- an early frontier validation effort
- a controlled burden-of-proof architecture
- a research-stage attempt to test whether bounded consciousness-like signals can be experimentally handled under disciplined conditions

These descriptions are strong enough to be meaningful, and restrained enough to remain honest.

---

## 5. What the Project Must Not Be Publicly Called Right Now

At the current stage, the project should **not** be publicly presented as:

- a finished consumer device
- a clinically validated diagnostic platform
- a therapeutic system
- a consciousness-reading machine
- proof that consciousness has been solved
- a completed AI-governance solution
- a fully validated public competition
- a medically actionable system
- a compliance-certified device unless that status has actually been earned under the canonical layer
- a platform already proven at the level of broad real-world deployment
- a finished public standard merely because materials are online

Those phrases do not merely overstate.

They mis-state the stage itself.

---

## 6. What May Be Said About the Current Stage

The following kinds of statements are generally appropriate now:

- “The current stage is pre-opening and research-stage.”
- “The present focus is kernel stabilization, not broad rollout.”
- “The work is non-therapeutic and non-diagnostic at this stage.”
- “The current burden is signal-interface reality, repeatability, leak control, and validation discipline.”
- “The program is designed to test whether a bounded interface can survive disciplined scrutiny.”
- “Success is not assumed.”
- “Negative and boundary results remain scientifically meaningful.”
- “The current question is narrower than the strongest future claim.”
- “Public language is intentionally tighter than ultimate ambition.”

These statements help readers locate the work accurately.

---

## 7. What May Be Said About “Consciousness-Like Signals”

The phrase “consciousness-like signals” may be used if it remains bounded.

It may appropriately refer to:

- reproducible patterns in measurable substrates
- patterns associated with controlled state transitions
- signals interpreted within the CAIS OE / RE / EE framework
- experimental attempts to distinguish state-linked signal structure under constrained conditions

It must **not** be used as a shortcut for:

- final metaphysical proof
- unrestricted consciousness measurement
- clinical mind-state diagnosis
- unrestricted psychological interpretation
- proof of agency, intention, or personhood
- proof that the project has solved the philosophy of consciousness

The phrase is useful only when it remains modest enough to stay technical.

---

## 8. What May Be Said About Sal-Meter

At the public-helper level, the following kinds of statements are generally appropriate:

- “Sal-Meter is a CAIS-linked research architecture for bounded state-sensitive signal work.”
- “The current Sal-Meter kernel program is research-stage and pre-opening.”
- “The project is focused on whether a bounded interface can be experimentally stabilized.”
- “The canonical meaning of Sal-Meter is fixed in the DOI layer.”

What should **not** be said casually:

- “Any consciousness-related biosensor is basically a Sal-Meter.”
- “This prototype is already a Sal-Meter” unless the relevant canonical and evidentiary conditions are actually met.
- “This system is Sal-Meter-compatible” without respecting the canonical designation and compliance layers.
- “This is our version of Sal-Meter” if such language weakens canonical definition or creates informal naming drift.

The public guide must preserve naming discipline, not loosen it.

---

## 9. What May Be Said About CAIS Compliance

The default public posture should be conservative.

Appropriate language includes:

- “This work is being explored in relation to CAIS-defined structures.”
- “This effort is intended to stay aligned with CAIS concepts and boundaries.”
- “Canonical CAIS compliance is governed by the proper canonical layer.”
- “Readers should not infer compliance status from helper documents or repository presence.”

Inappropriate language includes:

- “CAIS-compliant” without actual basis
- “basically compliant”
- “effectively compliant”
- “close enough to compliant”
- “compliant in spirit”
- “equivalent to CAIS”
- “same as canonical CAIS outputs” without disclosure and actual validity

Compliance is not a mood.
It is not a rhetorical compliment.
It is a bounded status condition.

---

## 10. What May Be Said About Validation

Public language about validation should remain exact.

Appropriate formulations include:

- “validation is ongoing”
- “the program is structured around validation pressure”
- “the architecture is being tested for repeatability, separability, drift, and leak control”
- “the current stage is about whether the bounded interface survives stricter scrutiny”
- “results may be positive, mixed, narrow, or negative”

Inappropriate formulations include:

- “validated” without qualification
- “scientifically proven” in a totalizing sense
- “already reproducible” if that claim exceeds the actual evidence base
- “objectively confirmed” when the actual result is still conditional, local, or preliminary
- “publicly validated” merely because materials are open

A validation phrase is only as honest as its narrowest unresolved burden.

---

## 11. What May Be Said About AI and Governance Relevance

The larger civilizational frame may be discussed.

But it must be discussed carefully.

Appropriate formulations include:

- “The long-term relevance may extend to AI evaluation, HCI, and governance questions if state-level measurement pathways become more mature.”
- “The project is motivated in part by the problem of state-level blindness in increasingly powerful systems.”
- “The governance relevance is part of the rationale, not proof of completion.”
- “The current technical burden remains narrower than the largest civilizational implications.”

Inappropriate formulations include:

- “AI governance is solved now.”
- “This device already fixes alignment.”
- “This architecture already gives civilization a working consciousness dashboard.”
- “The future implications are already implemented.”
- “Because the philosophical argument is large, the experimental pathway is already won.”

Large relevance may be part of the motivation.

It is not permission for present-tense overclaiming.

---

## 12. What May Be Said in Recruitment or PI Outreach

Recruitment and PI-facing language must remain especially careful.

Appropriate outreach language includes:

- “This is early.”
- “We do not assume the stronger claim.”
- “We are asking whether one bounded uncertainty can be reduced.”
- “Negative and boundary results remain meaningful.”
- “The current stage is research-stage, non-therapeutic, and pre-opening.”
- “A lab does not need to own the whole stack to matter.”
- “The question is whether your lab can reduce one real uncertainty.”

Inappropriate outreach language includes:

- “Join the breakthrough”
- “Help us prove consciousness”
- “This is already the future of medicine”
- “The hard question is already settled”
- “This is guaranteed to redefine the field”
- “This is ready for clinical use”
- “Participation implies official status”

Outreach should attract serious readers, not trigger cheap excitement.

---

## 13. What May Be Said in Presentations, Slides, or Public Talks

Public talks should follow the same discipline as the docs.

A good public presentation does the following:

- states the current stage clearly
- distinguishes helper material from canonical authority
- distinguishes architecture from validation
- distinguishes rationale from proof
- states the non-therapeutic boundary where relevant
- names unresolved burdens explicitly
- treats negative results as meaningful possibilities
- avoids performing certainty that does not yet exist

A weak public presentation does the opposite:

- uses polished visuals to imply maturity not yet earned
- treats helper diagrams as if they settle authority questions
- blurs exploratory work into conclusive proof
- uses audience excitement to hide unresolved technical burdens
- compresses the architecture into slogans that overstate its current achievement

Presentation elegance must never become an engine of overclaiming.

---

## 14. What May Be Said in Media, Interviews, or Social Posts

Short-form public language is where projects most easily become distorted.

Therefore media-facing and social language should stay especially simple.

Prefer language like:

- “research-stage”
- “pre-opening”
- “non-therapeutic”
- “bounded signal-interface work”
- “exploratory but disciplined”
- “testing whether a real interface can be stabilized”
- “negative results remain valid”

Avoid language like:

- “proven”
- “solved”
- “revolutionary” as a stand-in for evidence
- “first device to measure consciousness” unless the full basis truly exists and is canonically supportable
- “breakthrough” when what exists is still burdened early-stage work
- “validated” unless the scope and boundary of validation are made clear

Short formats magnify error.

That is why they need stricter discipline, not looser discipline.

---

## 15. What May Be Said About Results

When discussing results, use the smallest honest description first.

Prefer phrases such as:

- “suggestive”
- “bounded”
- “preliminary”
- “condition-dependent”
- “exploratory”
- “requires stricter replication”
- “survived this specific control set”
- “did not survive stricter controls”
- “boundary result”
- “local result under defined conditions”

Avoid phrases such as:

- “conclusive”
- “definitive”
- “fully demonstrated”
- “universally reproducible”
- “established fact”
- “clinically meaningful” unless that threshold has actually been crossed
- “generalizable” without real basis

Results should be described at the scale they actually earned.

---

## 16. A Practical Language Test

Before making any public statement, ask:

1. **Does this sentence describe the current stage, or the strongest hoped-for future stage?**
2. **Does this sentence imply compliance, designation, or proof that has not actually been earned?**
3. **Does this sentence blur helper explanation into canonical authority?**
4. **Does this sentence stay non-therapeutic and non-diagnostic where required?**
5. **Would a skeptical but fair PI consider this phrasing disciplined?**
6. **If the result later narrows or fails, will this sentence still look honest?**

If the answer to the last question is “no,” the claim is probably too large.

## 17. Preferred Phrasing vs Dangerous Phrasing

### Better

- “research-stage”
- “kernel-first”
- “pre-opening”
- “bounded signal-interface work”
- “consciousness-like signal measurement under constrained conditions”
- “non-therapeutic”
- “non-diagnostic”
- “validation remains ongoing”
- “the stronger claim is not yet assumed”
- “negative and boundary results remain valid”

### Worse

- “proved consciousness”
- “finished consciousness device”
- “validated diagnostic system”
- “AI-governance solution now”
- “officially compliant” without actual basis
- “Sal-Meter equivalent”
- “VCE/CRI/CFI-equivalent” without disclosure
- “this changes everything” as a substitute for evidence
- “the repository proves enough”
- “the question is already settled”

The difference is not style.

It is structural honesty.

## 18. What Contributors Must Especially Avoid

Contributors should not publicly:

- overstate their module’s meaning
- imply official status from collaboration alone
- present internal or local results as project-wide proof
- blur their implementation into the canonical structure itself
- market non-canonical indices as equivalents
- use the Sal-Meter name loosely
- imply that helper docs grant authority
- treat public enthusiasm as if it were validation
- turn the architecture into a personal branding surface detached from stage discipline

The program needs contributors who reduce risk, not contributors who amplify distortion.

## 19. What a Good Public Claim Looks Like

A good public claim has five traits:

- it is true at the current stage
- it is smaller than the speaker’s ambition
- it does not steal authority from the wrong layer
- it does not imply compliance or proof too early
- it could still be defended later even if the pathway narrows

Good public claims sound like:

- “We are testing a bounded interface.”
- “The current stage is early and research-focused.”
- “The hard part is not assumed solved.”
- “The program is structured to reduce uncertainty, not to manufacture certainty.”
- “We treat negative results as meaningful.”
- “The canonical layer governs naming, compliance, and definition.”

That is what mature speech sounds like in a frontier program.

---

## 20. What a Bad Public Claim Looks Like

A bad public claim usually has one or more of these traits:

- it is larger than the evidence
- it implies authority not granted
- it treats aspiration as achievement
- it compresses unresolved complexity into hype
- it invites misquotation
- it encourages others to repeat looser versions
- it creates reputational heat while reducing methodological clarity

Bad claims often sound exciting in the moment.

They age badly.

That is one reason they must be avoided early.

---

## 21. The Role of Silence

Sometimes the right public-language move is not better phrasing.

It is omission.

If a claim cannot yet be made honestly without layers of caveat,
do not force it into public-facing language just because a page, talk, or post feels empty without it.

Silence is sometimes stronger than premature compression.

Not every implication needs to be said early.

Not every possible future application needs to be named.

Not every large rationale needs to be restated in every venue.

Public restraint is not weakness.

It is part of how fragile work survives.

---

## 22. One-Line Summary

Public language must remain smaller than the strongest hope, tighter than the broadest narrative, and no larger than what the current authority and evidence layers can honestly support.

---

## 23. Final Position

A frontier project becomes believable not when it learns how to sound large, but when it learns how to remain exact under the pressure to sound large.

That is the function of this guide.

It exists so that the language does not get ahead of the kernel,
the echo does not outrun the evidence,
and the public face of the project remains disciplined enough that serious readers can still trust what it says.
