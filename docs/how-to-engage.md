# How to Engage — Sal-Meter Kernel Program

> This document explains how external readers, engineers, labs, and PI-level teams should engage with the Sal-Meter Kernel Program at the current stage.  
> It is an operational guide for disciplined engagement.  
> It does not grant participation status, compliance, naming rights, certification, or authority.

---

## 1. Why This Page Exists

Many people arrive at a repository like this and immediately ask the wrong first question:

- "How do I join?"
- "How do I build it now?"
- "Can I use the name?"
- "Is there funding?"
- "Is the competition already open?"

Those are understandable questions.  
But at the current stage, they are often too early.

This page exists to answer a more useful question:

**How should a serious reader engage with this project before broader opening has occurred?**

---

## 2. Current Rule of Engagement

At the present stage, the Sal-Meter program is **not broadly open as a public competition**.

That means engagement must be understood within a pre-opening, kernel-first structure.

The correct mindset is:

- understand first
- narrow second
- test bounded questions third
- avoid overclaiming throughout

This is not a hype environment.  
It is a boundary-discipline environment.

---

## 3. What Engagement Means at This Stage

At this stage, engagement means things like:

- reading carefully
- understanding the authority boundary
- identifying whether your expertise fits a bounded problem
- preparing better technical questions
- observing the current program direction
- avoiding premature claims
- resisting the urge to turn preparation into public certainty

Engagement does **not** mean automatic entry, public authorization, or naming freedom.

This page is especially relevant to:

## 5. The Three Valid Engagement Modes

At the current stage, there are three valid ways to engage.

## 5.1 Mode A — Read and Orient

This is the correct default mode for most readers.

In this mode, you should:

1. read the README
2. read the FAQ
3. read the roadmap
4. understand the authority boundary
5. understand that GitHub is operational, not canonical
6. identify whether your interest is technical, governance-related, or purely observational

This mode is not passive.
It is the foundation of all responsible later engagement.

---

## 5.2 Mode B — Evaluate Technical Fit

This mode is for readers who believe they may have real technical relevance.

Examples include:

- electrochemical characterization experience
- aptamer / molecular sensing experience
- drift / leak / baseline stabilization experience
- signal preprocessing / separability analysis experience
- bounded firmware / acquisition-control expertise
- validation protocol design capability

At this stage, the correct question is not:

**"How do I become part of the program?"**

The correct question is:

**"Do I actually fit one bounded problem strongly enough to matter?"**

---

## 5.3 Mode C — Form a Bounded Contribution Hypothesis

This is the most useful advanced engagement mode before broader opening.

A bounded contribution hypothesis sounds like this:

- "We may be useful for iodine redox baseline stabilization."
- "We may be useful for leak / cross-talk testing."
- "We may be useful for preprocessing and state-separability analytics."
- "We may be useful for narrow validation tooling."
- "We may be useful for interface reproducibility under constrained measurement conditions."

A poor engagement hypothesis sounds like this:

- "We can solve the whole thing."
- "We want to launch a product around this immediately."
- "We already understand the entire framework from one read."
- "We want to use the name now and refine later."

The narrower your first useful hypothesis, the better.

Before you ask serious questions, read in this order:

1. **README**

4. **Authority Boundary**

## 7. How Different People Should Engage

## 7.1 If You Are a PI

Your job at this stage is not to get excited publicly.

Your job is to decide privately whether your lab has a real bounded fit.

A disciplined PI should:

1. read the PI Quick Decision Pack
2. review the authority boundary
3. identify whether the lab has a real technical advantage in one narrow area
4. decide whether the fit is real before any outward posture forms
5. keep the distinction between helper documents and canonical authority clear

A PI should not assume that repository visibility equals invitation.

---

## 7.2 If You Are an Engineer

You should begin from failure modes, not from narrative.

The best engineer questions at this stage are things like:

- Where will drift break this?
- Where can false separation appear?
- What is likely to leak between channels?
- Which preprocessing assumptions are dangerous?
- What kind of stability claim is actually testable?

An engineer should focus on where the stack can fail honestly.

That is usually more valuable than trying to describe how impressive it could become.

---

## 7.3 If You Are an AI-Governance Researcher

You should treat this program as a proposed **research interface**, not as settled technology.

Your questions should focus on:

- why measurement visibility matters
- how the program relates to governability rather than hype
- how bounded signal architectures differ from grand consciousness claims
- where the proposed interface would strengthen or fail governance reasoning

Do not overread the existence of the program as proof that the final measurement problem is solved.

---

## 7.4 If You Are a General Observer

The correct role is simple:

- understand the stage
- avoid overclaiming
- watch the kernel-first logic carefully
- do not turn preparatory language into public certainty

That is already useful.

---

## 8. What a Good First Question Looks Like

A good first question is:

- bounded
- technical or structural
- stage-aware
- respectful of the authority boundary
- honest about uncertainty

Examples:

- "At the current stage, is the main concern repeatability, cross-talk, or interpretive overreach?"
- "Should this be understood as a kernel-stabilization effort rather than a public competition?"
- "Which layer is currently most structurally fragile?"
- "Is the main burden right now electrochemical stability, channel isolation, or broader validation logic?"

These are strong questions because they help clarify the real burden.

---

## 9. What a Bad First Question Looks Like

A bad first question is:

- too broad
- too early
- too commercial
- too authority-blind
- too naming-oriented

Examples:

- "How do I market this?"
- "Can I brand my system as Sal-Meter now?"
- "When is the token / startup / launch?"
- "Can I call this compliant if I build something similar?"
- "Can I use the public prose as proof of authorization?"

These questions are not merely unhelpful.
They often indicate that the reader has skipped the boundary discipline the project requires.

---

## 10. What You Should Prepare Before Reaching Out Seriously

Before any serious inquiry, you should prepare four things.

### 10.1 A stage-aware summary
Show that you understand the project is still pre-opening and kernel-first.

### 10.2 A bounded fit statement
State exactly where you think your expertise may matter.

### 10.3 A failure-aware mindset
Show that you understand negative results still matter.

### 10.4 Authority discipline
Show that you are not confusing GitHub prose with canonical authorization.

If you cannot state these clearly, you are not yet ready for high-quality engagement.
