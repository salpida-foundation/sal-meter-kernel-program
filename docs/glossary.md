# Glossary — Key Terms for Reading the Sal-Meter Kernel Program Correctly

> This glossary explains the core terms used across the Sal-Meter Kernel Program repository.  
> It exists to help researchers, scholars, engineers, PI-level readers, and serious observers read the project more accurately and with less friction.  
> It is an operational aid, not a replacement for canonical DOI-registered definitions where formal authority applies.

---

## 1. Why This Page Exists

As the repository grows, so does the number of terms that carry specific meaning inside the project.

Without a shared glossary, readers may:

- misunderstand the current stage
- confuse helper language with authority
- overread technical terms
- interpret internal sequence labels too loosely
- miss the difference between aspiration and earned status

This page exists to reduce that friction.

Its purpose is simple:

**to make the repository more readable by fixing the basic meaning of the terms that appear again and again.**

---

## 2. Reading Rule

When reading this glossary, remember:

- this page helps orientation
- this page does not override DOI-registered canonical definitions
- when a term has formal boundary significance, canonical documents prevail
- this glossary is meant to improve correct reading, not create a new authority layer

---

## 3. Core Structural Terms

## 3.1 Sal-Meter Kernel Program

The current pre-opening research-stage structure around the Sal-Meter project.

It is the present form of the work before any broader opening is justified.

The phrase emphasizes that the immediate burden is not public scale, but whether the kernel can become real, stable, and meaningful enough to deserve wider external expansion.

---

## 3.2 Kernel-First

A working principle meaning:

**first prove and stabilize the core measurement burden, then widen the field only if justified.**

Kernel-first rejects premature scaling, loose public opening, and narrative growth ahead of signal discipline.

---

## 3.3 Pre-Opening

A state in which the project is visible and increasingly legible, but not yet broadly open as a public competition or generalized external program.

Pre-opening means people may read, understand, prepare, and evaluate fit — but they should not assume broad authorization or final maturity.

---

## 3.4 Broader Opening

A later stage in which the field may be widened under controlled conditions if the kernel proves strong enough.

Broader opening does **not** automatically mean:

- certification
- compliance
- naming freedom
- clinical meaning
- unrestricted public implementation

It only means the project may be ready for a wider and more structured external relationship than it currently allows.

---

## 3.5 Operational Layer

The public working layer of the project.

In practice, GitHub functions as an operational layer:

- orientation
- structure
- navigation
- helper materials
- discussions
- public preparation

Operational does not mean authoritative in the canonical sense.

---

## 3.6 Canonical Authority

The formal authority layer of the project.

Canonical authority is fixed in DOI-registered documents and explicit canonical registry structures, not in GitHub helper prose.

If GitHub language and canonical language differ, canonical records prevail.

---

## 3.7 Helper Document

A document designed to help readers understand, navigate, or approach the repository correctly.

Helper documents may explain, orient, summarize, map, and clarify.

They do not bind meaning the way canonical documents do.

---

## 3.8 Legacy / Lower-Priority Working Document

A file that may still exist because it was useful earlier, but is no longer part of the main recommended path unless explicitly reactivated or upgraded.

Such documents are not necessarily wrong.
They are simply not the current primary entry surface.

---

## 4. Program Sequence Terms

## 4.1 External Layer-0

A separate pre-phase feasibility support track.

Layer-0 asks whether the upstream interface shows a stable enough signal window to justify deeper kernel work.

It is not the same as broader opening, and it is not proof of the full system.

---

## 4.2 Internal Phase 0

The phase focused on whether the G-axis can show controlled state-separable behavior under fixed baseline and protocol logic.

It is an early internal kernel-validation stage, not a public release stage.

---

## 4.3 Internal Phase 1

The phase focused on reproducibility of the I-channel.

Its burden is to determine whether the I-side of the interface can remain controlled, repeatable, and technically interpretable enough to move forward.

---

## 4.4 Internal Phase 2a

The phase focused on coexistence, channel integrity, and twin-structure testing.

A major question here is whether multiple channels can coexist without unacceptable leak, cross-talk, or false blending.

---

## 4.5 Internal Phase 2b

The phase in which early human pilot repeatability is examined under a bounded kernel structure.

The burden is still disciplined and limited.
This does not mean public clinical use, broad deployment, or generalized final validation.

---

## 4.6 Lock 1 — Interface Exists

A decision threshold asking whether the interface is experimentally real enough to be treated as more than narrative.

Lock 1 does not ask whether the system is famous, scaled, or commercially attractive.

It asks whether the interface genuinely exists in a defensible way.

---

## 4.7 Lock 2 — Interface Matters

A later threshold asking whether the interface matters enough to justify broader opening.

Existence alone is not enough.
The interface must also be meaningful and stable enough that widening the field does not merely amplify noise.

---

## 5. Scientific and Technical Terms

## 5.1 CAIS

A core framework term used in connection with the project’s interface architecture.

In practical repository reading, CAIS refers to the structured interface standard logic around how bounded signal interaction, measurement architecture, and disciplined interpretation are handled.

Readers should treat its exact formal meaning as canon-sensitive.

---

## 5.2 Sal-Meter

The project name tied to the kernel program and associated interface architecture.

Readers should not treat simple conceptual similarity as enough to justify public designation.

Naming and designation remain boundary-sensitive.

---

## 5.3 State-Separability

A practical question about whether different states can be distinguished in a bounded, reproducible, and technically honest way.

State-separability does not automatically mean full semantic understanding.
It means the project is asking whether structured difference is detectable without cheating, leakage, or inflated interpretation.

---

## 5.4 G-Axis

A shorthand term used for one part of the measurement burden in the current internal sequencing.

In repository reading, the G-axis should be understood as one bounded layer under investigation, not as a complete final account of the system.

---

## 5.5 I-Channel

A shorthand term for another part of the kernel burden, especially relevant in reproducibility and interface handling discussions.

The key reading rule is that the I-channel is part of a fragile technical burden, not a finished downstream product layer.

---

## 5.6 Drift

Unwanted change over time that can distort a system even when the underlying condition should remain stable.

In a project like this, drift is a central enemy because it can create false confidence, false separation, or unstable interpretation.

---

## 5.7 Leak

Structural contamination between channels, layers, or parts of the system.

A leak can make one part of the system appear informative when it is actually borrowing uncontrolled influence from elsewhere.

---

## 5.8 Cross-Talk

Hidden interaction between channels that creates false structure or misattributed signal.

Cross-talk often makes systems look richer than they actually are.

---

## 5.9 Baseline

The reference condition against which later differences are interpreted.

If the baseline is unstable or poorly defined, many downstream claims become weaker than they appear.

---

## 5.10 Preprocessing

All steps that transform raw signals before analysis or interpretation.

Preprocessing is powerful and dangerous.

It can clarify structure, but it can also create artificial cleanliness, false separability, or misleading downstream confidence.

---

## 5.11 Negative Result

A result that does not confirm the hoped-for effect, structure, or interpretation.

In this project, a negative result is still valuable if it is clean, bounded, and uncertainty-reducing.

A strong negative result may be more useful than a weak positive one.

---

## 5.12 Reproducibility

The degree to which results, structure, or behavior remain consistent under repeated or comparable conditions.

This is one of the deepest burdens in the program.

Without reproducibility, the project remains too loose to justify stronger claims.

---

## 6. Participation and Fit Terms

## 6.1 Bounded Contribution

A contribution narrow enough to be real, testable, and evaluable.

Examples include:

- drift characterization
- leak testing
- preprocessing sanity checking
- language tightening
- validation logic refinement

A bounded contribution is usually stronger than a grand but vague one.

---

## 6.2 Fit

The degree to which a person, lab, or team has a real and specific relevance to one burden in the project.

Fit is not the same as enthusiasm.
Fit means capability meets a real uncertainty.

---

## 6.3 Serious Reader

A person who does not stop at attraction, dismissal, or symbolic admiration.

A serious reader tries to understand:

- the stage
- the authority boundary
- the technical burden
- what is and is not being claimed
- where their own relevance might actually begin

---

## 6.4 PI / Lab Lead

A principal investigator or laboratory decision-maker evaluating whether the project is worth disciplined attention from a real research environment.

In this repository, PI-level reading is meant to be careful, bounded, and methodologically mature.

---

## 7. Claims and Boundary Terms

## 7.1 Public Claims

The language used to describe the project in outward-facing ways.

Because the project is still early, public claims must remain strictly proportional to stage and evidence.

---

## 7.2 Claims Discipline

The practice of preventing language from outrunning proof.

Claims discipline means not describing the project as more finished, more open, more certified, or more semantically settled than it actually is.

---

## 7.3 Naming Rights

Permission or legitimacy to use protected or boundary-sensitive designations.

Repository visibility alone does not grant naming rights.

---

## 7.4 Compliance

A formal status that must not be inferred casually.

GitHub prose, repository presence, or conceptual similarity do not by themselves establish compliance.

---

## 7.5 Certification

A stronger formal status than mere visibility or helper documentation.

Nothing in the ordinary existence of a repository should be mistaken for certification.

---

## 7.6 Non-Diagnostic

A boundary indicating that the repository and its current helper materials should not be read as providing diagnostic meaning.

---

## 7.7 Non-Therapeutic

A boundary indicating that the repository and its current helper materials should not be read as conferring therapeutic use, treatment logic, or clinical meaning.

---

## 7.8 Stage-Accurate Language

Language that remains faithful to what the project actually is now, rather than what it may someday become.

This is one of the most important habits for reading and writing around the repository.

---

## 8. Human and Motivational Terms

## 8.1 Love for Humanity

Not decorative sentiment, but the conviction that technical systems should not become more powerful while remaining blind to the beings they affect.

In the repository’s working culture, love for humanity becomes structure, discipline, boundary, and refusal to overclaim.

---

## 8.2 Love for the Future

A motive that refuses to leave future human conditions to blind optimization, empty ethics language, or uncontrolled technical acceleration.

In this project, love for the future is meant to become method.

---

## 8.3 Care as Structure

The idea that real care must eventually take form as:

- protocol
- clarity
- rigor
- protection against misuse
- tighter language
- honest limits
- disciplined opening

This is a central theme across many repository pages.

---

## 8.4 Technical Honesty

The refusal to pretend that a fragile signal is stronger than it is, a boundary wider than it is, or a proof more finished than it is.

Technical honesty is one of the deepest values in the repository.

---

## 9. Practical Reading Reminder

If a term feels important, ask three questions:

1. Is this term being used operationally or canonically?
2. Is it describing the current stage or a future possibility?
3. Is the language narrower than hype, or broader than proof?

Those questions will prevent most reading mistakes.
