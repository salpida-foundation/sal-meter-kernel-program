# PI Quick Decision Pack — Sal-Meter Kernel Program  
## A Go / No-Go Brief for PIs and Lab Leads

> This brief is for principal investigators, laboratory directors, and senior research decision-makers.
> It is designed to help you decide—within about 30 minutes—whether the Sal-Meter Kernel Program is worth serious attention from your lab at the current stage.
> It is an operational helper document, not a canonical authority document.

---

## ONE SENTENCE FOR YOU

With your lab’s strength in sensing, molecular interfaces, signal processing, validation, or state-related protocols, this is a rare chance to test whether a new class of bounded consciousness-like signal interface can become experimentally real—knowing that success could open a new measurement frontier, and failure could still define a scientifically valuable boundary under your lab’s own authorship.

## 2. What stage this project is in

The Sal-Meter program is **not currently in broad public opening**.

At present, it is a **pre-opening, kernel-first, research-stage program**.

That means the immediate burden is not:

- market launch
- certification
- broad public participation
- productization
- public naming freedom

The immediate burden is whether a bounded CAIS-aligned molecular-electrochemical kernel can be made:

- real
- repeatable
- bounded
- state-separable under honest conditions
- strong enough to justify broader opening later

A PI should evaluate the project at exactly that level.

---

## 3. What you would actually do

Not “measuring consciousness” in the final philosophical sense.

What you would actually do is test the following question:

> **Using your lab’s real methods, can a bounded interface produce reproducible signal behavior associated with consciousness-like state transitions strongly enough to survive disciplined evaluation?**

In practical terms, this may involve:

- electrochemical interface work
- molecular sensing work
- signal acquisition and preprocessing
- state-transition protocol design
- validation and separability logic
- negative-control design
- bounded theoretical / governance framing tied to measurable structure

Your role is not to produce metaphysical conclusions.

Your role is to determine:

- what works
- what does not
- where the signal is fragile
- where the architecture survives
- where interpretation must remain narrow

If one or more of the following sound like your lab, you are already close to a real fit.

### A. Electrochemistry / biosensor / chemical-biology labs
You have experience with:

- novel sensing interfaces
- redox systems
- electrode behavior
- biomolecular sensing
- signal stability under experimental conditions

### B. Signal processing / ML / statistics labs
You have experience with:

- noisy experimental data
- preprocessing and normalization
- classification discipline
- validation logic
- separability analysis
- leakage detection

### C. Neuroscience / cognitive science / state-protocol labs
You have experience with:

- sleep
- meditation
- acute stress
- anesthesia-adjacent state protocols
- psychophysiological modulation or observation of conscious-state changes

### D. Governance / STS / philosophy-of-science groups
You have experience with:

- measurement frameworks
- institutional and interpretive boundaries
- claims discipline
- governance implications of emerging technical categories

You do **not** need to own the full stack.

A lab that can responsibly own **one real layer** is already relevant.

## 5. The rule environment you step into

If your lab engages seriously, you are stepping into the following environment.

### 5.1 Canonical authority lives in DOI documents
GitHub and helper documents are explanatory and operational.

They are **not** the final source of authority.

### 5.2 The standard itself is not privately ownable
The core shared measurement structure is not meant to become a closed monopoly layer.

### 5.3 This is not a prize-first or hype-first structure
The program is not built around spectacle.

It is built around burden, milestones, validation, and disciplined progression.

### 5.4 Success is not assumed
The premise is **not** that signal separation is already proven.

The premise is that serious attempts at separation should be tested openly and honestly.

### 5.5 Negative and boundary results are valid
A conclusion such as “no effect,” “weak effect,” or “effect only under narrow conditions” still counts as meaningful scientific output here.

If that environment is unacceptable, this is probably not the right stage for your lab.

## 6. Questions you should ask yourself now

If any one of the following is clearly “No,” it is safer to defer active engagement for now.

- Do we have real personnel and methods for at least one bounded implementation layer?
- Can we work in an environment where comparison, replication, and validation matter more than narrative?
- Can we allocate limited effort for a 3–6 month exploratory burden without destabilizing the lab?
- Are we willing to treat negative and boundary results as meaningful outcomes?
- Can we keep our public language narrower than our internal curiosity?
- Are we comfortable entering a pre-opening program rather than a ready-made mature platform?

This is not about whether the project is “good.”

It is about whether **your lab and this burden fit each other now.**

---

## 7. Three risks you must accept

### 7.1 Short-term reward risk
There is no guaranteed fast payoff in:

- money
- clinical translation
- consumer relevance
- short-term prestige

If you need immediate external reward, this may feel too early.

### 7.2 Boundary-result risk
Your conclusion may be:

- no reproducible effect
- only narrow effect
- unresolved ambiguity
- a failure mode that closes a pathway rather than opens one

Those outcomes still matter here.

### 7.3 Non-exclusive structure risk
The core public structure is not designed to become one lab’s private semantic territory.

What your team produces remains yours.
But the broader shared structure is not here to grant monopoly symbolism.

If exclusivity is the main motive, this is not the correct project phase.

---

## 8. What you gain instead

What you gain here is **position**, not exclusivity.

That may include:

- being among the first labs to test a bounded consciousness-like signal architecture seriously
- being cited later as a lab that handled the burden experimentally instead of only conceptually
- generating protocols, datasets, validation logic, or negative controls that later work must reckon with
- shaping the boundary of what becomes testable and what must still be rejected

Ten years from now, the meaningful question may not be only “who talked about consciousness?”

It may also be:

**who actually helped force the measurement burden into disciplined form?**

This brief asks whether your lab wants to be on that list.

---

## 9. IP and authorship

Authorship of your papers, code, datasets, prototypes, schematics, and lab-generated results remains with you and your team.

The shared structure does **not** mean your lab gives up authorship.

In short:

- **your results are yours**
- **shared standards remain shared**
- **helper documents do not erase your contribution**
- **canonical boundaries do not claim your lab’s output**

This reduces one of the most common PI anxieties.

---

## 10. A realistic minimal participation model

A serious lab does **not** need to begin by trying to own everything.

A realistic starting model is:

### Team
- 1 PI or senior researcher
- 1 researcher / engineer / postdoc / advanced student

### Duration
- roughly 3–6 months

### Scope
Own **one module** that overlaps strongly with your current capability

Examples:

- stabilizing an electrochemical baseline
- characterizing drift
- testing leak / cross-talk risk
- adapting an existing sensing setup to CAIS-compatible conditions
- designing preprocessing sanity checks
- building validation logic for state-separability
- designing a bounded state-transition protocol
- contributing theory / governance framing that remains operational and disciplined

This should become either:

- a sharpened pilot if it works, or
- a clear boundary result if it does not

Both are scientifically valuable.

## 11. Yes / No checklist

If three or more answers are “Yes,” a serious exploratory conversation inside your lab is reasonable.

- [ ] Can we identify at least one overlapping module with our current work?
- [ ] Is a limited 3–6 month exploratory burden not a long-term loss for our lab?
- [ ] Are we willing to produce outcomes that may include failure or constraint?
- [ ] Does this frontier connect meaningfully to our long-term curiosity or direction?
- [ ] Can we enter this without overclaiming publicly?
- [ ] Can we distinguish helper documents from canonical authority?

If only one or two answers are “Yes,” it is better to treat the program as something to watch and revisit later.
